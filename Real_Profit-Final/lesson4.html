+ 1. How is a trend formed ?

- A trend is formed by the movement of prices. Prices are determined by the supply and demand in the market.

- Since the supply and demand in the market constantly change, the market trend also changes accordingly.

- A trend can strengthen, weaken, or be broken and reversed, forming a new trend.

+ 2. The 3 Main Types of Trends ?

* Uptrend

- An uptrend occurs when prices continuously form higher highs and higher lows compared to previous ones.

* Downtrend

- A downtrend occurs when prices form lower lows and lower highs compared to previous ones.

* Sideways Trend (Sideway):

- A sideways trend happens when the market does not exhibit a clear trend direction.

+ 3. Trend and How to Identify Trends.

- In the financial market, trends can be categorized into two types: Trend and Strong Trend. Understanding these distinctions and the structure of trends is essential for effective analysis.

* Components of Trends

  • BULLISH Trend:

    • A bullish trend is characterized by rising prices, where higher highs and higher lows are formed.
    • It consists of two main components:

      • BEARISH Wave (Main Wave):The primary upward movement of prices, driving the trend.

      • Corrective Wave (Sub Wave): A temporary downward movement or consolidation within the larger bullish trend, often referred to as a bearish wave.

  • Bearish Trend:

    • A bearish trend is characterized by falling prices, where lower highs and lower lows are formed.
    • It consists of two main components:
      • BEARISH Wave (Main Wave): The primary downward movement of prices, driving the trend.
      • Corrective Wave (Sub Wave): A temporary upward movement or consolidation within the larger bearish trend, often referred to as a bullish wave.

* Identifying Trends

- To identify a trend, observe the market for consistent patterns of price movement, such as:

  • For a bullish trend, look for a series of higher highs and higher lows.
  • For a bearish trend, look for a series of lower highs and lower lows.
  • During corrective waves, price movements will temporarily counteract the main trend before resuming the dominant direction.

* When is a Trend Considered BROKEN ?

- A trend is considered broken when the closing price violates the critical levels of the trend:

  • For an Uptrend:

    • The trend is broken when the closing price falls below the lowest point (the trough) that created the most recent highest peak.
    • This indicates that the buyers are losing control, but it does not necessarily confirm the formation of a downtrend.

  • For a Downtrend:

    • The trend is broken when the closing price rises above the highest point (the peak) that created the most recent lowest trough.
    • This signals weakening selling pressure but does not guarantee the start of an uptrend.

* Key Points to Remember

  • A broken trend suggests that the existing trend's momentum has weakened, and the market is at a potential inflection point.
  • A broken uptrend does not immediately imply a downtrend is forming, and vice versa. The market may enter a sideways trend (consolidation) or show signs of indecision before confirming a new trend direction.
  • Confirmation of a new trend requires consistent higher highs and higher lows (for an uptrend) or lower highs and lower lows (for a downtrend).
+ 4. Why Trade WITH the Trend, NOT Against the Trend ?

- The foundation of any market trend lies in supply and demand dynamics. Understanding these forces helps traders recognize the importance of aligning their trades with the prevailing trend.

* The Market as a Battlefield

  • The market is akin to a battlefield with two opposing sides: the buyers and the sellers
  • In an uptrend the buyers have the upper hand, consistently outnumbering the sellers and driving prices higher.
  • Conversely, in a downtrend, the sellers dominate, pushing prices lower.

- As a trader, you act as an observer entering this battlefield. Aligning with the stronger side (the trend) increases your chances of success.

* The Risks of Trading Against the Trend

  • Higher Probability of Loss: Going against the trend is like driving against traffic. While you may navigate safely at times, the likelihood of a collision (loss) is significantly higher.
  • Reduced Edge: Trading against the prevailing market momentum puts you at a disadvantage, as you're fighting the dominant supply or demand force.

* Conclusion

- A professional trader must adopt the mindset of "the Trend is my Friend." This principle serves as a guiding mantra to navigate the market with discipline.

  • Trading with the trend means aligning your positions with the prevailing market forces, increasing the probability of success.
  • Trading against the trend introduces unnecessary risk, with a higher likelihood of loss.

- Ultimately, the choice is clear: be the driver on the right side of the road, and let the trend lead you toward consistent profits.

+ 5. Entry Price Area in a Trend.

- Trading successfully in trending markets requires a deep understanding of trends and well-timed entries and exits. This is the core principle of "Trading According to the Trend."

* Purpose

- In an uptrend

  • The price forms higher peaks and higher troughs compared to previous levels.
  • To maximize profits, the optimal strategy is to:
    • Buy at the troughs (when the price dips within the trend).
    • Sell at the peaks (when the price reaches a high point).

- By entering trades at favorable points in the trend, traders increase their profit potential while minimizing risks.

* Requirements

  • Choose an Appropriate Time Frame (TF):

    • Select a time frame that aligns with your trading style (e.g., daily for swing trading or hourly for intraday trading).
    • Stick to this time frame as your primary reference for identifying trends and making decisions.

  • Understand the Trend:

    • Develop a clear understanding of how to identify trends (uptrend, downtrend, or sideways).
    • Use tools like trendlines, moving averages, and market structure to confirm the trend.

* Important Notes

  • Avoid Trading Without a Clear Trend:

    • If the market lacks a definitive trend (e.g., sideways movement), wait for clarity before taking a position.
    • Trading in unclear conditions increases the risk of false signals.

  • Do Not Chase the Trend:

    • Avoid entering trades impulsively at random points within the trend, especially at peaks where the price is already extended.
    • Wait for pullbacks (retracements) to align your entry with the trend's continuation.
+ 6. Trading Method: Steps to Execute.

Step 1: Identify the Price Correction Area

  • A correction area is the zone where the price temporarily moves against the main trend before resuming its dominant direction.

  • Use the following criteria to confirm a correction area:

    • Clear Peaks: Clear Peaks: The subsequent peak must create a new, distinct high in an uptrend or a new low in a downtrend. Avoid counting insignificant movements like single candles.
    • Look for consistent price movements that reflect a pullback or retracement within the trend.

Step 2: Wait for the Price to Enter the Correction Area

- Once the correction area is identified, do not act impulsively. Instead:

  • Wait for the price to retrace into the predefined correction area.
  • Monitor the price action closely within this zone to confirm potential entries.
  • Use indicators (e.g., Fibonacci retracement levels, support zones, or trendlines) to validate the correction area.

- By patiently waiting for the price to fall into the correction area, traders can align their entries with high-probability setups that take advantage of the trend's continuation.

* Key Tip:

- The goal is to enter trades when the correction ends and the price shows signs of resuming the main trend.

- This approach minimizes risk and enhances profit potential by leveraging the natural ebb and flow of market movements.

+ 7. Identifying the Exact Buying Point in a Correction Zone.

Understanding the Corrective Wave in an Uptrend

  • In an uptrend, the corrective wave originates from the peak and ends at the trough that follows.
  • The optimal buying point is at the trough of the trend, where the corrective wave concludes and the main trend is likely to resume.

- To identify this buying point, the focus must be on pinpointing where the corrective wave ends, signaling the best entry for a buy trade.

+ 8. Using Two Time Frames (TF).

- This method leverages two time frames to effectively analyze and enter trades:

  • Main Time Frame (Main TF):

    • Purpose: Identify the overall market trend.
    • Example: If the main TF is H1, you'll focus on identifying the broader uptrend and correction zones within it.
    • Action: Mark the peaks and troughs, and identify the corrective wave within the trend.

  • Secondary Time Frame (Secondary TF):

    • Purpose: Zoom in to analyze the structure of the corrective wave on the main TF.
    • Example: If the main TF is H1, use M15 as the secondary TF.
    • Observation:
      • The corrective wave on the main TF becomes a downtrend on the secondary TF (opposite the main trend).
      • This downtrend on the secondary TF provides insight into the movement of the correction.
+ 9. Steps to Identify the Optimal Buying Point.

  • On the Main TF:

    • Identify the corrective wave within the uptrend.
    • Mark the area where the price has retraced and is likely to form a trough.

  • On the Secondary TF:

    • Zoom into the corrective wave to observe its structure (now visible as a downtrend).
    • Look for reversal patterns (e.g., double bottom, bullish engulfing candlestick) or signals that the corrective wave is losing momentum.
    • Use additional tools like support levels or Fibonacci retracement (e.g., 50% or 61.8%) to confirm the end of the corrective wave.

  • Confirm the Buying Point:

    • Wait for confirmation on the secondary TF that the downtrend is reversing.
    • Enter a buy trade near the trough, ensuring that the main trend on the main TF aligns with your entry.
+ 10. Key Notes.

- Always align trades with the main TF's trend.

- Use the secondary TF to fine-tune entries and achieve precision.

- Avoid impulsive trades—ensure there are clear reversal signals before entering.

+ 11. Setting Entry, Stop Loss, and Take Profit.

* Key Perspective

- We only enter a trade when the correction wave (downtrend on the secondary TF) has ended and the price shows signs of resuming the main trend (uptrend on the main TF).

  • The correction wave is treated as a trend of its own (a downtrend in this context).
  • The end of the correction wave does not guarantee a trend reversal, but it provides the opportunity for an optimal buy entry aligned with the main trend.
+ 12. Steps to Set Entry, Stop Loss, and Take Profit.

  • Entry

    • * Criteria for Entry:

    - Wait for confirmation that the correction wave (downtrend) has ended.

    - Signs to confirm the end of the correction wave:

    • Price breaks above a key resistance level formed within the correction wave.
    • Bullish reversal patterns (e.g., double bottom, bullish engulfing candlestick, or hammer) on the secondary TF.
    • Momentum indicators (e.g., RSI moving above 50 or MACD bullish crossover) signaling upward movement.

    * Action: Enter the trade as soon as these confirmations align, ensuring the entry point is as close as possible to the end of the correction wave.

  • Stop Loss

    * Purpose: To limit potential losses in case the trend does not resume or reverses unexpectedly.

    * Placement:

    • Place the stop loss just below the lowest point of the correction wave (the trough on the main TF).
    • For additional safety, consider adding a small buffer below the trough to account for market noise.

  • Take Profit

    * Purpose: To secure gains by exiting at a favorable price point.

    * Placement:

    • Identify the next resistance level on the main TF as the first take profit target.
    • Alternatively, calculate a specific risk-to-reward ratio (e.g., 1:2 or 1:3) based on the distance between the entry point and the stop loss.
    • For trends with strong momentum, consider scaling out profits at multiple levels:
      • First TP: At the previous peak of the main trend.
      • Second TP: At an extension level (e.g., 127.2% or 161.8% Fibonacci extension of the corrective wave).
+ 13. Example Workflow.

  • Main TF (H1):

    - Identify the uptrend and mark the correction zone.

  • Secondary TF (M15):

    - Monitor the correction wave.

    - Confirm the end of the downtrend with bullish signs (e.g., breakout, reversal pattern).

  • Set Parameters:

    - Entry: Enter after confirmation of the trend resumption.

    - Stop Loss: Below the trough of the correction wave on the main TF.

    - Take Profit: At the next resistance level or based on a risk-to-reward ratio.

+ 14. Important Notes!

  • Patience is crucial—do not rush into a trade without confirmation.

  • Always maintain a favorable risk-to-reward ratio to ensure long-term profitability.

  • Adjust stop loss and take profit levels dynamically if the trend gains momentum or if new resistance/support levels form.
